
Modern self-driving cars and vehicles such as the Tesla Model S & Tesla Model Y rely heavily on a technology called “Computer Vision”, which acts as the eyesight of the vehicle to read different traffic signs on the road to make safe driving decisions that are faster and more accurate than a human can. However, a grave problem arises when these signs are exposed to nature causing them to erode and fade away, making it so that the vehicles are not able to recognize these signs. This happens because such vehicles are trained on perfectly clean traffic signs but this falls apart completely once, they leave the factory and walk on the real-life roads of a country that, for example, faces heavy monsoon such as India. This weathering confuses the AI model causing it to misread or entirely ignore such signs. While researchers spend a huge unreasonable amount for collecting real-life images of such weathered signs to train their models, I created a software tool which automatically generates realistic wear and tear on a perfectly normal sign image to solve this problem efficiently.
 
The first step of this project was to collect and create a dataset using real-world traffic signs. Instead of relying on artificial computer graphics, I collected three real traffic sign images from the three main categories: a "No Entry" sign (mandatory rules), a "School Ahead" sign (cautionary warning), and a blue "Parking" sign (driver information). To make sure that the AI system could analyse and work with each image effortlessly, each image was cropped into a standardized and clean square format.

As I started with only Three base images, I built a digital weathering engine using a specialized python library/module called “Albumentations”. This engine allowed me to simulate real life wear & tear, erosion simultaneously on my computer automatically. I programmed it to do two main things to the clean photos – It first shifts the brightness of the image and drops the colour saturation randomly using “ColorJitter” which mimics how sunlight erodes sign paint over a period of time. Second, I used “CoarseDropout” to randomly delete small blocks of pixels in the image all across the sign. This simulates physical damage such as erosion, rusting, tampering etc. By running the three original images through the model, I was able to generate Sixty completely new and unique, weathered variations.

The final step of the experiment/project was to build the Artificial brain to read and analyse these images. I utilized PyTorch to construct a custom CNN (Convolutional Neural Network). A CNN is a type of deep learning model (DLM) designed to analyse a set of images layer by layer by breaking them down. The first few layers of the network focus on figuring out the basic edges and colour changes, the middle layers combine those edges into shapes (circles and triangles), and the final layer acts as a decision maker in deciding what category the sign belongs to and what the name of the sign is. I fed the Sixty uniquely generated weathered images to the AI model in small batches (four at a time) allowing the AI to learn from its mistakes and correct itself.

During the experiment, the artificial intelligence model’s performance was tracked through five different training cycles, known as “epochs”. In the first epoch, the model achieved a training accuracy of 78.3% with a loss score of 0.6604, meaning it was still figuring out how to differentiate between the blurred or rusted shapes and the backgrounds. However, by the very next epoch, its accuracy jumped to a perfect 100.0%, while the loss score dropped dramatically down to 0.0415. For the remaining three epochs, the model easily maintained the 100.0% accuracy, ultimately lowering its final loss score to an almost perfect 0.0001. These numbers prove that the artificial brain learned to identify the main features of the traffic signs incredibly fast.
These results show that using digital wear and tear is an incredibly powerful tool for training self-driving vehicle systems. Even though the images were heavily edited and eroded up with random rust patches and faded colours, the CNN successfully learned to ignore all that visual editing. It did this by focusing on the underlying structures rather than just relying on bright, clean paint. Because the model achieved perfect accuracy so quickly on a smaller dataset, it proves that self-driving cars do not need millions of expensive real-world photos to learn how to handle broken infrastructure; they just need targeted data during their training phase.

In conclusion, this project demonstrates successfully that software-based data augmentation is a highly efficient and low-cost alternative to manual dataset collection for automated vehicles. By building a digital weathering engine using “Albumentations”, clean traffic signs were transformed into realistic, environmentally degraded & eroded variations. The custom PyTorch Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)  trained on this data until it reached 100.0% accuracy by the second epoch, proving that artificial intelligence can easily learn to see past severe physical damage when given the right training and data set. In the future, this work can be expanded by simulating other harsh conditions like heavy monsoon fog, night-time glare, or physical dents, ultimately helping self-driving cars navigate unpredictable real-world roads much more safely.
